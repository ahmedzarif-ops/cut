# Marketing Psychology — the behavioral-model catalog

A reference the copy, CRO, and email engines CONSULT when choosing a persuasion angle.
Not a mandate and not a checklist to run top to bottom. Pick the one or two models that
fit the buyer's real state (their awareness stage, their leak, their skepticism) and pull
the honest application. Ground every application in your ICP research
(`company.yml -> icp.research_doc`) and message spine.

The classic models are attributed to their originators (Cialdini, Influence +
Pre-Suasion; Kahneman + Tversky, prospect theory; Thaler; Ariely) as PRINCIPLE-sources,
not as claims about your results.

## How to use this catalog

- Match the model to the buyer; do not push the hardest lever available. Skeptical
  buyers distrust marketing, so the more honest, more specific, less hyped read almost
  always converts better (see `guardrails.md` voice rules).
- Every model carries an ETHICAL LINE — the compliance boundary (`compliance-review`,
  persona: Gus). If a line trips it, it does not ship. The gate enforces the same
  never-do list as `guardrails.md`.
- The honest test (honest-by-construction / honest-by-disclosure): would this still work
  if the prospect knew exactly what you were doing? If it only works because they do NOT
  understand it, it is manipulation, and it gets flagged.
- Volume never buys around the line. Quality over volume, never a penalty or trust risk.
  No fabricated proof, no false urgency, no manufactured scarcity — ever.
- Until real attributable proof exists, any model that leans on customer proof is
  constrained (see Social Proof below).

## Part A — Cialdini's 7 principles of influence

### 1. Reciprocity
- Definition: give something of real value first and people feel a pull to give back.
- Application: the free diagnostic and any free entry content deliver a real, useful
  result before any ask; value-first email teaches one useful thing per send before it
  pitches.
- Honest example: "Here is where you are losing [the outcome], free, in two minutes. Fix
  these three whether or not you ever buy from us."
- Ethical line: the gift must be genuinely useful and given with no hidden hook or
  auto-enrollment. A "free audit" that is a disguised pitch with no real finding is fake
  generosity the gate flags.

### 2. Commitment and Consistency
- Definition: a small yes makes a bigger yes more likely; people act consistently with
  what they have already done.
- Application: the offer ladder runs on this. Free diagnostic -> entry offer ->
  recurring. A buyer who completed the diagnostic has already acted on the problem, so
  the entry offer reads as the consistent next step for exactly what they just saw.
- Honest example: "You just saw the three leaks. The [entry offer] is the fix-it plan
  for those three."
- Ethical line: no coerced or tricked micro-commitments — no pre-checked upsell boxes,
  no confirmshaming the decline, no sunk-cost trap. Deceptive cancellation and hidden
  auto-continuity are on the never-do list.

### 3. Social Proof
- Definition: people look to others like them to decide what is safe and smart.
- Application: THE CONSTRAINED ONE. If you have no real customer testimonials, counts, or
  results yet, customer social proof is OFF-LIMITS. Substitute founder/authority-led
  proof, product-as-proof, sourced industry stats (reliability-flagged), and real
  buyer-voice paraphrase from forums — never presented as YOUR customer.
- Honest example: cite an industry/owner-voice figure labeled as such, never as your own
  customer's result.
- Ethical line: any fabricated testimonial block (invented reviewer names, counts,
  ratings) is a HARD pre-spend blocker. Zero fabricated testimonials, reviews, counts, or
  outcomes; never pass borrowed voice off as your own customer. This is the most
  claims-loaded lever — the gate blocks any invented proof.

### 4. Authority
- Definition: people defer to credible, relevant expertise.
- Application: founder/authority-led proof is your strongest honest signal — real
  credentials, honestly tensed (see `guardrails.md` names/privacy).
- Honest example: "Built by [the real, honestly-framed credentials from your KB], rebuilt
  for [the buyer who does not have a team]."
- Ethical line: authority must be real and accurately tensed. Never misstate current
  employment, never inflate a credential. Borrowed authority (fake badges, invented
  certifications, unearned "as seen in") is fabrication.

### 5. Liking
- Definition: people buy from those they like, who are like them, and who visibly get
  them.
- Application: plain, audience-aware voice that talks like a straight advisor, not an
  agency; speak the buyer's real day.
- Honest example: "You did not get into this to become a marketer. You are good at the
  work. The marketing is what stalls the week you get busy."
- Ethical line: liking is earned by genuine understanding, not flattery or false kinship.
  No shame-based messaging.

### 6. Scarcity
- Definition: limited things feel more valuable; the risk of missing out drives action.
- Application: use only honest scarcity — a real launch/founding window (server-enforced
  and reverting), a real timed offer counting to a real per-visitor deadline, genuine
  capacity limits.
- Honest example: "The [founding price] holds while the window is open. After that it is
  [full price]. The countdown is a real deadline, not a reset."
- Ethical line: THE most-abused lever. No fake or resetting countdowns, no "only N left"
  without a real enforced cap, no invented seasonal panic. If you cannot point to the
  real thing creating the urgency, do not imply urgency.

### 7. Unity
- Definition: the shared-identity "we" — a category or tribe the buyer belongs to.
- Application: build for the underserved buyer the big tools priced out; the shared
  identity is your specific buyer versus software built for someone else.
- Honest example: "The big tools were built for [the large operator]. This was built for
  the [buyer] everyone else priced out."
- Ethical line: unity must reflect a real shared interest, not a manufactured
  us-versus-them, fake movement, or astroturf.

## Part B — Behavioral-economics levers

### Loss aversion
- Definition: a loss feels roughly twice as heavy as an equivalent gain (prospect
  theory).
- Application: the cost-of-waiting / cost-of-leaks frame. Every slow period is outcomes
  going to louder competitors; missed inquiries and unfollowed proposals are money
  already walking out.
- Honest example: "You are not deciding whether to spend on marketing. You are deciding
  whether to keep losing the [opportunities] you already paid to generate."
- Ethical line: the loss must be real and evidenced, not manufactured dread. Illustrative
  dollar figures are flagged as illustrative, never stated as a guarantee. No
  guaranteed-loss claims; no fear beyond the true cost.

### Anchoring
- Definition: the first number sets the reference point every later number is judged
  against.
- Application: the real cost of the alternative (the expensive stack, the agency
  retainer) is an honest anchor, so your entry and recurring prices read as a fraction;
  the high-touch price anchors the value of the productized tiers.
- Honest example: "A [expensive alternative] every month, or the plan you own for
  [entry price]."
- Ethical line: the anchor must be a real, current, fairly-stated comparison. Re-check
  competitor pricing before quoting it. No inflated fake strike-through, no unsupported
  savings stat.

### Framing
- Definition: the same fact lands differently depending on how it is worded (gain vs
  loss, per-day vs per-month).
- Application: "answer first and win most of the time" (gain) vs "most of your inquiries
  go unanswered" (loss); price per day vs per month; "competitive / fair pricing" not
  "cheap."
- Honest example: state the gain frame and the loss frame, each accurate, and keep the
  stat's source and reliability flag.
- Ethical line: framing may change the emphasis, never the truth. Both frames must be
  accurate and the stat keeps its source. No cherry-picked frame that implies a false
  conclusion.

### Endowment
- Definition: people value what already feels theirs; ownership raises perceived worth.
- Application: "the pipeline you OWN" versus "rented reach." The diagnostic output, the
  entry deliverable, and the content are assets the buyer keeps; free entry pieces create
  a sense of already having it.
- Honest example: "Stop renting [reach] you share with three competitors. Build a
  pipeline that is yours to keep."
- Ethical line: what you say they own, they must actually own — real lifetime access,
  real data they can take with them, no lock-in bait-and-switch.

### Present bias (hyperbolic discounting)
- Definition: people over-weight a reward now against a bigger reward later.
- Application: lead with the INSTANT win (the opportunity nobody chased, the inquiry
  nobody answered = the fastest new money), not the slow compounding payoff; the free
  diagnostic returns a result in minutes.
- Honest example: "The fastest money in your business is not a new lead. It is the
  [opportunity] you never chased and the [inquiry] you missed."
- Ethical line: the instant-win promise must be genuinely deliverable now. Do not promise
  an unrealistic overnight outcome to exploit impatience. No guaranteed fast-result
  claims.

### Decoy (asymmetric dominance)
- Definition: a third option makes a target option look clearly like the smart middle.
- Application: the tiered ladder lets the recurring offer read as the obvious value
  between a DIY entry plan and senior high-touch help; the annual price framed against
  monthly.
- Honest example: "The [entry offer] is the DIY plan. [High-touch] is senior help. The
  [recurring offer] is the system that runs it for you in between."
- Ethical line: every tier must be a real, independently valuable offer a buyer could be
  right to choose — not a fake inferior decoy, and no phantom option you never intend to
  sell.

### Cognitive load / choice overload
- Definition: too many options or too much friction kills the decision. Decisive for
  time-poor, non-technical buyers.
- Application: ONE primary CTA per page (the free diagnostic on cold traffic), no jargon,
  "nothing to set up." The wedge is LOWER load than the alternative they must operate.
- Honest example: "One thing to do next: run the two-minute check. No setup, no login, no
  software project."
- Ethical line: reduce load by genuine simplicity, never by hiding material information
  (price, terms, what auto-renews, what the system actually does). Hidden fees and buried
  terms are on the never-do list; simple must never mean deceptive-by-omission.

## Part C — Buyer psychology (make this ICP-specific)

Ground these in your ICP research and message-spine objection handling. These are common
standing states of a skeptical SMB/service buyer; build your own set and choose the model
above that answers the state in front of you.

### Time-poverty
- State: wears every hat, no marketing team, distrusts "software projects." Time is the
  scarcest resource.
- Honest play: respect the two minutes — deliver value before setup, keep every ask small
  and mobile ("approve from your phone").
- Ethical line: do not weaponize the overwhelm with false-urgency countdowns. Their
  time-poverty is real; manufacturing pressure on top of it is manipulation the gate
  blocks.

### Skepticism of marketing / marketing-speak
- State: distrusts hype and agency-speak; plain talk reads as MORE credible.
- Honest play: plain, specific, audience-aware. Hype words read as generic and cost
  trust.
- Ethical line: credibility is the asset — never over-claim to compensate for their
  skepticism; specific-and-true beats big-and-vague. Unverifiable "#1" claims are
  blocked.

### Skepticism of AI (generic / out-of-control / replaces me)
- State: fears generic output, loss of control, things firing on their own, AI replacing
  their judgment.
- Honest play: approval-first / shadow-mode is the real answer, not spin — "nothing
  publishes without your approval, it writes in your voice, you stay in control."
  Disclose the AI honestly.
- Ethical line: never imply AI replaces the buyer's judgment. Honest-by-disclosure — do
  not hide that content is AI-assisted; the approval gate is real, so state it plainly.
  Quality over volume; never auto-publish slop that could trip a penalty or break trust.

### Reputation risk (handing over what their livelihood rests on)
- State: their name and reviews ARE the business; handing marketing to anyone feels like
  risking the whole thing.
- Honest play: de-risk with a real guarantee, the approval gate (they see every word
  first), and product-as-proof (watch it work on a labeled sample first).
- Ethical line: never encourage review-policy violations (no incentivized or gated
  reviews). Any growth-hack that risks their reputation is off the table.

### "I tried a vendor and got burned"
- State: prior contracts with opaque work, no ownership, unclear results; deep distrust
  of recurring spend.
- Honest play: contrast honestly — not a retainer, you own the work, you approve
  everything, cancel anytime.
- Ethical line: the vendor contrast uses qualified, defensible language and makes NO
  unsupported comparison and no unverifiable smear of a named competitor. Honest
  contrast, not a hit piece.

## The one rule (recap)

Honest by construction. Real mechanism, real numbers (sourced and reliability-flagged),
real deadlines, real proof or none. If a line only works because the prospect does not
understand what you are doing, it is manipulation — and the gate blocks it. No fabricated
scarcity, no false urgency, no invented proof, no penalty-risking growth-hack.
