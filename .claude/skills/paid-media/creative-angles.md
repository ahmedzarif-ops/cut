# Creative Angle Library

An angle is a stable strategic idea; hooks are the scroll-stopping front line. Build
your own angle library grounded in your ICP research (`company.yml -> icp.research_doc`)
and message spine — the archetypes below are the reusable shapes, not your copy. Every
angle and every hook must be voice-clean (per `guardrails.md`) and carry ZERO fabricated
proof: no invented customer names, counts, or revenue. Pull example hooks into copy and
vary them per the testing hierarchy (see `testing-and-measurement.md`).

## How to build an angle

Each angle needs: a **core idea** (the strategic bet), the **pain** it speaks to (in the
buyer's own words, from real voice-of-customer), the **best offers** to pair it with, and
2-3 **hooks** (standalone scroll-stoppers). Draft several, then test the highest-level
unknown first per the concept hierarchy.

## Angle archetypes (adapt each to your ICP)

These generalize across most SMB/service verticals. Fill the brackets from your market.

### 1. Better [work/product], weaker visibility
- Core idea: the best provider is not always the one winning online.
- Pain: quality does not matter if the buyer cannot find or trust you before they
  choose.
- Best offers: diagnostic, entry offer.

### 2. Stop renting [leads/reach], own the pipeline
- Core idea: a rented/shared channel is not a pipeline; it is rented lottery tickets.
- Pain: paying for reach you may not win, competing on the same lead, building the
  platform's asset instead of your own.
- Best offers: diagnostic, alternative-to-the-incumbent content, entry offer.

### 3. Where [the outcome] leaks before conversion
- Core idea: the buyer has already judged you before they ever reach out.
- Pain: weak proof, an unclear site, slow response, no follow-up silently cost deals.
- Best offers: diagnostic.

### 4. The follow-up gap
- Core idea: most deals do not lose on price; they die because no one followed up.
- Pain: sent quotes/proposals become forgotten opportunities; most losses are to
  silence, not a "no."
- Best offers: recurring offer, consultation.

### 5. The agency / vendor-retainer alternative
- Core idea: do not pay thousands a month to rent someone else's playbook.
- Pain: contracts, unclear work, opaque results, no ownership. Use qualified,
  defensible language; make no unsupported comparisons.
- Best offers: entry offer, recurring offer.

### 6. Your presence should match your work
- Core idea: you do premium work; your online presence should make that obvious.
- Best for: high-ticket / considered-purchase segments.
- Best offers: diagnostic, entry offer, recurring offer.

### 7. Automation without loss of control
- Core idea: the system does the repetitive work; you approve every word.
- Pain: fear of generic output, loss of control, bad posts, technical complexity.
- Best offers: recurring offer, demo/sample.

### 8. The consistency problem
- Core idea: your marketing does not need another burst of effort; it needs to keep
  moving while you are busy with the work.
- Best offers: recurring offer.

### 9. Category + location visibility
- Core idea: being listed is not the same as showing up when someone searches
  [category] in [place].
- Best offers: diagnostic, entry offer.

### 10. The instant win (recover what you already have)
- Core idea: the fastest new money is not a new lead; it is the opportunity nobody
  followed up and the inquiry nobody answered.
- Pain: silent opportunities die unfollowed; inquiries go to whoever responds first.
- Channel role: a first-class lead angle for cold prospecting. Content-led creative
  moves to a background, retargeting-support role, not the cold lead.

## Hook-bank discipline

Keep a running bank of your strongest standalone openers. Each hook must:

- Stop the scroll in one line, in the buyer's own words.
- Be honest — no implied guarantee, no fabricated number.
- Map to one angle above and one offer.
- Be varied and rotated per the fatigue rules (`testing-and-measurement.md`).

Generic hook shapes that travel well (fill the brackets from your market):

- "Your competitors are not better than you. They are just easier to find."
- "Paying for [rented channel] is not the same as owning a pipeline."
- "The [proposal] you sent last week may not be dead. It may just be unfollowed."
- "If your phone is not ringing, it may not be your work. It may be this."
- "Most lost [deals] are lost to silence, not a no."
- "Your work is premium. Your [online presence] is not, and it is costing you the big
  jobs."
- "The fastest money in your business is the [opportunity] you never chased and the
  [inquiry] you missed."
- "Watch the system grade your [site] and draft a month of marketing. You approve every
  word."
