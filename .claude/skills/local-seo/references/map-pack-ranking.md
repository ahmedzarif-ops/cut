# Map-Pack Ranking — the triad, deep, plus service-area pages

Open this to reason about *why* a client ranks (or doesn't) and to build the localized pages that support the map pack without tripping the doorway-page spam policy. Sourcing note: the three-factor model is Google's own ("Improve your Business Profile ranking on Google," support.google.com); the doorway-page policy is Google Search's spam policy. Verify current wording before quoting to a client.

## The triad — each factor, what moves it, what to touch first

### 1. Proximity (mostly fixed — don't fight it, don't fake it)

- Google favors businesses physically close to the searcher. A business can't be everywhere; results shift as the searcher moves.
- **You can barely move this honestly**, and every dishonest lever (fake address, extra pins in other towns, keyword-stuffed location) is a suspension trigger that doesn't durably work. Don't.
- What you *can* do: (a) win the queries winnable from the true location; (b) set an accurate, high-value **service area** so the profile is eligible across the real footprint; (c) build **service-area pages** (below) to earn organic + relevance signals for edge towns you legitimately serve.

### 2. Relevance (the fastest honest win — start here)

- How well the profile + site match the query. This is where most quick wins live.
- Levers, in order of impact: **primary category** (biggest), secondary categories, services list, business description, service-area coverage, and **on-site content** that matches trade + city.
- First moves: fix the primary category to the most specific accurate one, complete services, ensure the site has real service + location content. See `gbp-optimization.md` for the profile side.

### 3. Prominence (the grind — compounds over time)

- How well-known/trusted the business is. The lever a business can most actively pull.
- Drivers: **reviews** (count + velocity + recency + rating + response rate — the single biggest active lever; see `citations-and-reviews.md`), **citation consistency** (NAP across the web), **real backlinks + local press/mentions**, and the **website's organic authority** (which is why the blog engine feeds local).
- First moves: turn on an honest post-job review flow; clean up NAP/citations; earn real local links (suppliers, associations, sponsorships, local news). Slow but durable.

**The one-line strategy:** proximity is near-fixed, so **win relevance fast (categories + services + on-site content) and grind prominence forever (reviews + citations + links).**

## Map pack vs organic — when each wins

| Query type | Winner | Why | Optimize |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| "[service] near me," "emergency [service]," "[service] [city]" | **Map pack** | High-intent, ready-to-buy, local | GBP + reviews + relevance |
| "how much does X cost," "A vs B," "signs you need X" | **Organic** | Research/comparison intent, feeds trust before the call | Blog engine, service pages |
| Branded ("[business name]") | **Both** | They already know the client | Complete profile + site |

They compound: organic authority is a **prominence** signal that lifts the map pack, and the map pack sends the **highest-intent** clicks. Run both; sequence **map-pack first** for fastest revenue.

## Service-area & city pages — legitimate, not doorway spam

A business serving multiple towns needs localized pages to rank organically and reinforce local relevance. The failure mode is **doorway pages** — a Google spam-policy violation that gets a site penalized.

### The line

- **Doorway (banned):** a template with the city name swapped in, near-duplicate across dozens of towns, thin, no unique value, built only to funnel search traffic to one destination. Do NOT do this. Do NOT mass-generate city pages to chase a footprint — it risks a Google penalty and the client's trust (quality over volume, always).
- **Legitimate:** each page is about a place the client **genuinely serves** and can speak to with real substance.

### What a real service-area page carries

- **Unique local proof:** actual projects completed in that town/area (photos, brief case notes) — never fabricated.
- **Local specifics:** relevant permits, climate/seasonal issues, local code notes, common problems for that area (e.g. hard water, older housing stock, seasonal demand).
- **Neighborhoods / ZIPs** genuinely served, honest response-time expectations.
- **Town-specific FAQs**, the services offered there, and a clear local CTA.
- **Original copy** per page — no city-swap templating. If you can't write something genuinely distinct and true about a town, don't build the page for it yet.

### Build discipline

- **Only for areas truly served.** No page for a town the client can't service.
- **A handful, done well > dozens, thin.** One strong page beats ten empty ones — for both ranking and trust.
- **Hand the AEO layer to `ai-seo`:** FAQ/definition blocks, schema (LocalBusiness / Service / FAQPage), machine-readable structure. Local page = real local substance; ai-seo = the extractable structure on top.

## Measuring the map pack

- **Local grid / geo-ranking check:** ranks vary by the searcher's exact spot, so a single "what do I rank" check lies. Use a local-grid tool (samples rank across a grid of points in the service area) or manually search the money queries from inside the area. Track a small set of **money queries** monthly.
- **Headline metric:** map-pack rank movement on the top money queries. Secondary: review count + velocity + rating, GBP calls/direction requests/clicks (GBP performance insights), organic ranks for service-area pages.
- Report the map-pack rank as the story; reviews and citations as the inputs driving it.
